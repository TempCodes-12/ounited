Sail Rock

A lone pinnacle rising out of open water — one of the Gulf of Thailand's most celebrated dive sites, and one of the few places in the region where whale sharks still show up.

The rock everyone's heard of

Sail Rock isn't an island — it's a single pinnacle of rock pushing out of the open water roughly halfway between Koh Samui and Koh Tao. From the deck of the catamaran it looks like almost nothing: a jagged point, some birds, open sea in every direction. Under the surface it's a different story entirely.

The rock drops steeply into deep water on all sides, with walls covered in soft coral and schooling fish. A chimney passage running vertically through the rock — accessible from about 8 meters down — is one of the more unusual features in the Gulf. Whale shark sightings here are real and documented, particularly from March through May, though never guaranteed.

What to expect

01

Open-ocean conditions — the crossing and the site itself are more exposed than the island routes. Some swell is normal.

02

Diving from the catamaran directly, with no beach or land to explore — this is a water-only stop.

03

Marine life density that rewards patience — barracuda, trevally, batfish, and grouper are regulars at the rock.

04

The chimney passage is one of the site's highlights — a surreal vertical swim through the rock itself.

On the water

Sail Rock works best paired with Koh Tao or Koh Nang Yuan on a longer full-day route — it sits naturally in the path between Koh Samui and the northern islands. As a standalone stop it's short on variety above the waterline, so if you're not diving or snorkeling, combining it with another destination makes the most of the day.
